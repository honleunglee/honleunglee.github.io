Stiefel manifold

June 21, 2026

This article states and proves some facts about the Stiefel manifold [1]. The Stiefel manifold is named after Swiss mathematician Eduard Stiefel. Many results and proofs come from [2].

Given integers $n\geq p\geq 1$, we define the Stiefel manifold by $$ V(n,p) := \{X\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p} \ : \ X^\top X = I\}. $$ The columns of a matrix $X\in V(n,p)$ are $p$ orthonormal vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Our first result shows that $V(n,p)$ is a smooth manifold.

Theorem 1.

The Stiefel manifold $V(n,p)$ is a compact embedded submanifold of $\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$ of dimension $np - \frac{1}{2}p(p+1)$. Morever, its tangent space at $X\in V(n,p)$ is given by $$ T_X V(n,p) = \{U\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p} \ : \ X^\top U + U^\top X = 0\}. $$

Proof. Let ${\rm Sym}(p)$ be the vector space of $p\times p$ real symmetric matrices. Consider the smooth map $F:\mathbb{R}^{n\times p} \rightarrow {\rm Sym}(p)$ given by $F(X) := X^\top X - I$. One has $V(n,p) = F^{-1}(\{0\})$ which implies $V(n,p)$ is closed in $\mathbb{R}^{n\times k}$. In addition, $V(n,p)$ is bounded in $\mathbb{R}^{n\times k}$, hence it is compact. Let $X,U\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$ and $t\in \mathbb{R}$. Since $$ F(X+U) = (X+tU)^\top (X+tU) - I = (X^\top + tU^\top)(X+tU) - I = t(X^\top U + U^\top X) + t^2(U^\top U)+ X^\top X - I, $$ the differential of $F$ at $X$ is $dF(X)[U] = X^\top U + U^\top X$. For any $X\in V(n,p)$ and $Z\in {\rm Sym}(p)$, one has $$ dF(X)\left[ \frac{1}{2}XZ \right] = X^\top \frac{1}{2}XZ + \left[ \frac{1}{2}XZ \right]^\top X = \frac{1}{2}IZ+\frac{1}{2}IZ = Z. $$ This means $dF(X)$ is surjective for any $X\in V(n,p) = F^{-1}(\{0\})$, and thus $0\in {\rm Sym}(p)$ is a regular value of $F$. By the regular level set theorem [3, Corollary 5.14], and since $\dim(\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}) = np$ and $\dim({\rm Sym}(p)) = \frac{1}{2}p(p+1)$, we get the first result. As $F$ is a defining map for $V(n,p)$, by [3, Proposition 5.38] we know $T_X V(n,p) = {\rm ker}(dF(X)) = \{U\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p} \ : \ X^\top U + U^\top X = 0\}$. ■

The next result is an alternate expression of the tangent space of the Stiefel manifold.

Theorem 2.

The tangent space of the Stiefel manifold $V(n,p)$ at $X\in V(n,p)$ is given by $$ T_X V(n,p) = \{XZ + X_\perp A \ : \ Z \in \mathbb{R}^{p\times p} \text{ is skew-symmetric, } A\in \mathbb{R}^{(n-p)\times p} \} $$ where $X_\perp\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times (n-p)}$ is a matrix whose columns span the orthogonal complement of the column span of $X$.

Proof. Since any vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a linear combination of the columns of $X$ and the columns of $X_\perp$, any $n\times p$ matrix $U\in T_X V(n,p)$ can be expressed as $U = XZ+X_\perp A$ for some matrices $Z\in \mathbb{R}^{p\times p}$ and $A\in \mathbb{R}^{(n-p)\times p}$. By Theorem 1, we know $X^\top U + U^\top X = 0$ which implies the equality $$ X^\top (XZ+X_\perp A) + (Z^\top X^\top + A^\top X_\perp^\top) X = 0. $$ As $X^\top X = I$ and $X^\top X_\perp = 0$, we get $Z+Z^\top = 0$ which means $Z$ is skew-symmetric. This proves one inclusion. The converse of the above argument holds because if $Z\in \mathbb{R}^{p\times p}$ is skew-symmetric and $A\in \mathbb{R}^{(n-p)\times p}$ then $U=XZ+X_\perp A$ satisfies $X^\top U + U^\top X = 0$. This finishes the proof. ■

Notice that if $n=p$ meaning $V(n,p)$ is the real orthogonal group $O(n)$ then the above tangent space formula simplifies to $$ T_X O(n) = \{XZ \ : \ Z \in \mathbb{R}^{p\times p} \text{ is skew-symmetric} \} $$ for any $X\in O(n)$.

Since $\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$ is a Riemannian manifold with the standard inner product $$ \langle X, Y \rangle = {\rm tr}(X^\top Y) \text{ where } X,Y\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p}, $$ the Stiefel manifold $V(n,p)$ is a Riemannian submanifold with the same inner product restricted on $V(n,p)^2$. Then we have the following formula for the normal space of $V(n,p)$.

Theorem 3.

The normal space of the Stiefel manifold $V(n,p)$ at $X\in V(n,p)$ is given by $$ (T_X V(n,p))^\perp = \{XB \ : \ B \in \mathbb{R}^{p\times p} \text{ is symmetric} \} $$

Proof. For any symmetric matrix $B\in \mathbb{R}^{p\times p}$, skew-symmetric matrix $Z\in \mathbb{R}^{p\times p}$ and $A\in \mathbb{R}^{(n-p)\times p}$, one has $$ {\rm tr}((XB)^\top (XZ+X_\perp A)) = {\rm tr}(B^\top X^\top XZ + B^\top X^\top X_\perp A) = {\rm tr}(BZ) = 0, $$ since $X^\top X = I$, $X^\top X_\perp = 0$, $B^\top = B$, $Z^\top = -Z$ and ${\rm tr}(BZ) = {\rm tr}((BZ)^\top) = {\rm tr}(Z^\top B^\top) = -{\rm tr}(ZB) = - {\rm tr}(BZ)$. This proves the inclusion $\supset$. As the right hand side is a vector subspace of dimension $\frac{1}{2}p(p+1)$ which equals to the dimension of $(T_X V(n,p))^\perp$, the inclusion is indeed an equality. ■

Next, we compute the projections of any matrix $Y\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$ on the tangent and the normal spaces of $V(n,p)$.

Theorem 4.

For any matrix $Y\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$, its projections on $T_X V(n,p)$ and $(T_X V(n,p))^\perp$ are given by $$ {\rm proj}_{T_X V(n,p)}(Y) = X {\rm skew}(X^\top Y) + (1-XX^\top) Y, \ {\rm proj}_{(T_X V(n,p))^\perp}(Y) = X{\rm sym}(X^\top Y), $$ where ${\rm sym}(A) := \frac{1}{2}(A+A^\top)$ and ${\rm skew}(A) := \frac{1}{2}(A-A^\top)$ denote the symmetric part and the skew-symmetric part of a matrix $A$ respectively.

Proof. Pick $X_\perp$ in a way that stacking $X$ alongside with $X_\perp$ forms a matrix in $O(n)$. It follows that $XX^\top + X_\perp X_\perp^\top = I$. Any matrix $Y\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$ is a sum of two terms $$ Y = XX^\top Y + (I-XX^\top) Y = [X{\rm skew}(X^\top Y) + X_\perp (X_\perp^\top Y)] + X{\rm sym}(X^\top Y), $$ so that by Theorems 2 and 3, the first term $X{\rm skew}(X^\top Y) + X_\perp (X_\perp^\top Y)$ belongs to $T_X V(n,p)$ and the second term $X{\rm sym}(X^\top Y)$ belongs to $(T_X V(n,p))^\perp$. Hence the result follows. ■

Consider the Euclidean connection (i.e., standard directional derivative) $D$ on $\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$. Then the Riemannian connection $\nabla$ on the embedded submanifold $V(n,p)$ is given by $$ \nabla_U \eta = {\rm proj}_{T_X V(n,p)}(D\eta(X)[U]) = X {\rm skew}(X^\top D\eta(X)[U]) + (1-XX^\top) D\eta(X)[U] $$ for any $X\in V(p,n)$, $U\in T_X V(n,p)$ and vector field $\eta$ on $V(p,n)$.
