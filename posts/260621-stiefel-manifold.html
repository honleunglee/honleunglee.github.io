Stiefel manifold

June 21, 2026

This article states and proves some facts about the Stiefel manifold [1]. The Stiefel manifold is named after Swiss mathematician Eduard Stiefel.

Given integers $n\geq p\geq 1$, we define the Stiefel manifold by $$ V(n,p) := \{X\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p} \ : \ X^\top X = I\}. $$ The columns of a matrix $X\in V(n,p)$ are $p$ orthonormal vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Our first result shows that $V(n,p)$ is a smooth manifold.

Theorem 1.

The Stiefel manifold $V(n,p)$ is a compact embedded submanifold of $\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$ of dimension $np - \frac{1}{2}p(p+1)$. Morever, its tangent space at $X\in V(n,p)$ is given by $$ T_X V(n,p) = \{U\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p} \ : \ X^\top U + U^\top X = 0\}. $$

Proof. Let ${\rm Sym}(p)$ be the vector space of $p\times p$ real symmetric matrices. Consider the smooth map $F:\mathbb{R}^{n\times p} \rightarrow {\rm Sym}(p)$ given by $F(X) := X^\top X - I$. One has $V(n,p) = F^{-1}(\{0\})$ which implies $V(n,p)$ is closed in $\mathbb{R}^{n\times k}$. In addition, $V(n,p)$ is bounded in $\mathbb{R}^{n\times k}$, hence it is compact. Let $X,U\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p}$ and $t\in \mathbb{R}$. Since $$ F(X+U) = (X+tU)^\top (X+tU) - I = (X^\top + tU^\top)(X+tU) - I = t(X^\top U + U^\top X) + t^2(U^\top U)+ X^\top X - I, $$ the differential of $F$ at $X$ is $dF(X)[U] = X^\top U + U^\top X$. For any $X\in V(n,p)$ and $Z\in {\rm Sym}(p)$, one has $$ dF(X)\left[ \frac{1}{2}XZ \right] = X^\top \frac{1}{2}XZ + \left[ \frac{1}{2}XZ \right]^\top X = \frac{1}{2}IZ+\frac{1}{2}IZ = Z. $$ This means $dF(X)$ is surjective for any $X\in V(n,p) = F^{-1}(\{0\})$, and thus $0\in {\rm Sym}(p)$ is a regular value of $F$. By the regular level set theorem [2, Corollary 5.14], and since $\dim(\mathbb{R}^{n\times p}) = np$ and $\dim({\rm Sym}(p)) = \frac{1}{2}p(p+1)$, we get the first result. As $F$ is a defining map for $V(n,p)$, by [2, Proposition 5.38] we know $T_X V(n,p) = {\rm Ker}(dF(X)) = \{U\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times p} \ : \ X^\top U + U^\top X = 0\}$. ■
